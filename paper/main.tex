\begin{abstract}
\setstretch{1.2}%
We present nine independent statistical tests on a corpus of $N = 2{,}742$
clean inscriptions drawn from the Mahadevan (1977) concordance
(textnums 1001--9905; Mohenjo-daro, Harappa, Chanhudaro, Lothal,
Kalibangan, and West Asian sites).
The \textbf{Suffix Theorem} (Analysis~1) shows sign M342 occupies the
terminal position in 68.0\% of its occurrences across 2,742 inscriptions,
with a binomial $p = 1.70 \times 10^{-222}$ against the uniform null.
The \textbf{Positional Entropy} test (Analysis~2) confirms a drop of
0.558~bits from position~0 to position~1, the mathematical signature of
an open initial vocabulary constrained by a terminal grammar
(\emph{previously pending at pilot scale; now confirmed}).
A \textbf{Co-occurrence Cluster} test (Analysis~3) shows signs M267, M99,
and M342 co-occur at $5.5\times$ above chance ($\chi^2 = 523$,
$p \approx 0$; \emph{previously pending; now confirmed}).
The \textbf{Agglutination Ratio} (Analysis~4) shows sign M48 precedes M342
at $8.97\times$ above independence, consistent with suffix attachment.
A \textbf{Grammar Engine} (Analysis~5) built from the full bigram
transition matrix identifies 398 distinct signs, 598 statistically
significant transitions ($\geq$3 occurrences), and a three-way functional
partition: terminal signs (M342, M176, M328), strictly medial signs
(M99 at 97.0\%, M347 at 98.9\%), and initial-biased signs (M267 at
79.5\%, M95 at 93.3\%)---consistent with morphological role
differentiation.
A \textbf{Cross-Site Markov Consistency} test (Analysis~6) trains the
transition matrix on Mohenjo-daro ($n = 1{,}347$) and scores Harappa
($n = 1{,}103$) inscriptions against it.
Harappa achieves log-likelihood $+6.19$ above a 1,000-permutation random
baseline ($Z = 50.12$, $p = 0$).
Crucially, the grammar fit decays with geographic distance: Harappa
($+6.19$), Lothal ($+6.01$), Chanhudaro ($+5.29$), Kalibangan ($+4.05$),
West Asian finds ($+1.08$)---a spatial gradient consistent with
a geographically structured writing system, with highest inter-site
grammatical similarity between the two largest sites.
A \textbf{Direction Grammar Test} (Analysis~7) resolves the prior RTL
assumption: direction data from the \texttt{indusarrays} Firestore
collection classifies 2,360 inscriptions as RTL and 175 as LTR.
LTR perplexity (97.68) is 7,887$\times$ below random ($p = 1.03 \times
10^{-54}$, Cohen's $d = 1.634$), confirming that grammatical
dependencies survive direction flip even as exact word order degrades
($13.3\times$ perplexity increase).
A \textbf{Motif--Sign Correlation} test shows sign M113 at
$37.9\times$ positional enrichment on elephant-motif seals in a
recurrent medial formula across two sites; no semantic inference
follows from enrichment alone.
M172--M304 appears in an identical eight-sign sequence on all 7
attested Kalibangan UNICORN seals (PMI\,=\,7.94; $n = 7$,
single site; sample-size caution warranted).
A \textbf{Geographic Cluster} test identifies M328 as 90.1\%
Harappa-concentrated and M93 as 100\% Chanhudaro-concentrated;
154 structural substitution synonyms are recovered, with M328,
M12, and M343 confirmed as cross-site positional equivalents.
Signs M304 and M328 have exact shape-matches in Tamil Nadu Iron Age
graffiti documented by \citet{Rajan2025}; no functional or descent
continuity is implied.
All code is open-source and reproducible.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% ============================================================

The Indus Valley Script (\textsc{ivs}) remains one of the world's last
undeciphered writing systems, comprising over 5,000 inscriptions from the
mature Harappan phase (c.\ 2600--1900 \BCE).
Whether the script encodes a natural language---as opposed to
administrative tokens, heraldic marks, or guild symbols---is a foundational
open question \citep{Farmer2004,Sproat2014}.

Prior computational work has approached this question through
conditional entropy \citep{Rao2009}, sign-frequency statistics
\citep{Mahadevan1977}, and rebus analysis \citep{Parpola1994}.
\citet{Nair2026} showed on 1,916 ICIT inscriptions that no non-linguistic
generator reproduces the full Indus statistical profile.

This paper moves beyond the binary language/non-language question to
ask: \emph{if the script encodes language, what structural properties
does that language have?}

We make nine independently falsifiable structural claims (Sections~3--11),
each grounded solely in sign-position and sign-transition statistics
with no phonetic assumptions.
Phonetic readings are isolated in Section~11 and are not necessary for
any structural result.

\section{Corpus and Methods}
\label{sec:corpus}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Corpus}

The corpus is derived from the Mahadevan (1977) concordance database
as made available in machine-readable form, covering textnums 1001--9905
across all major Indus Valley Civilisation sites.

Raw records: 3,572 sign-rows (including multi-line inscriptions encoded
as separate rows).
After grouping multi-line rows by inscription and applying the erosion
mask, the clean corpus comprises $N = 2{,}742$ inscriptions.

\paragraph{Corpus scope relative to ICIT.}
The ICIT database \citep{Fuls2010} catalogues approximately 5,000 text
instances, a figure that includes fragmented tablets, potsherds, and
multi-line texts counted as independent string entries.
This study prioritises structural integrity over corpus breadth.
By applying a strict erosion mask and grouping by physical artifact
rather than individual text string, we produce a high-integrity subset
of $N = 2{,}742$.
For Markov transition matrices and positional entropy calculations,
including statistically padded or fragmented sequences introduces noise
that artificially inflates bigram variances.
The $2{,}742$-inscription clean corpus represents the largest purely
intact structural dataset analysed in the Indus computational
literature to date.
Notably, this study uses Fuls' independent terminal-marker labelling
(TMK for M342, assigned from frequency statistics without phonetic
assumptions) as an external validation anchor for Analysis~1---confirming
that our structural results converge with independent findings from the
broader ICIT corpus.

\paragraph{Erosion mask.}
Any inscription containing sign~0 (the Mahadevan conventional symbol for
an illegible or lost passage) is excluded from all statistical tests.
This is a conservative strategy: 703 inscriptions (19.5\% of grouped
records) are dropped.
Survivorship note: the mask disproportionately removes short inscriptions
where a single illegible gap destroys the entire record; the clean corpus
therefore skews slightly toward longer texts (mean length 4.13 signs vs.\
an estimated 3.8 signs in the full corpus).
All results should be interpreted in this context.

\paragraph{Directionality.}
Seals are carved in mirror-image; impressions read right-to-left (RTL).
All sequences are stored in linguistic order (initial morpheme first,
terminal last).
\emph{Known limitation:} the direction field was not available in the
machine-readable export; RTL is assumed for all inscriptions.
This assumption is correct for $>$95\% of records (standard seals);
the remaining $<$5\% (LTR tablets, graffiti) will have reversed sign
order, potentially injecting a small noise signal into bigram statistics.

\noindent\textit{Note on sign numbering:}
The terminal sign (jar pictogram; Parpola P385; Wells 520; ICIT TMK)
carries number \textbf{M342} in the primary Mahadevan (1977)
concordance database as digitised in the \texttt{indusarray} collection.
Some secondary literature cites M137 for the same sign;
M137 is Mahadevan's numbering for a \emph{variant form} of the jar
pictogram (the ``jar with base''), which was separated from the main
jar sign M342 in the 1977 concordance.
The two numbers refer to distinct entries in the concordance;
M342 is the dominant high-frequency terminal sign confirmed by
1,190 occurrences in this corpus.
All analysis in this paper uses M342 consistently.

\paragraph{Sign notation and bias invariance.}
Sign notation is dictated entirely by data provenance: the
machine-readable export natively encodes signs using Mahadevan (1977)
integers.
Translating to an alternate sign list (e.g., Wells 2006) prior to
computation would introduce unnecessary crosswalk mapping errors.
Because all statistical tests in this paper measure positional
distributions and transition probabilities between integer states,
the results are strictly invariant to sign-label nomenclature---relabelling
M342 as Wells~520 or any other identifier leaves every entropy value,
$p$-value, and $Z$-score unchanged.
Cross-system validation is achieved by mapping final results to
Wells/ICIT identifiers (Table~\ref{tab:crosssys}), not by
preprocessing the corpus through a secondary sign list.

\section{Analysis 1 --- Suffix Theorem}
\label{sec:analysis1}
% ============================================================

\paragraph{Hypothesis.}
If M342 is a grammatical terminal morpheme, it must appear
disproportionately in the final position of inscription sequences.

\paragraph{Test.}
One-tailed binomial test.
$H_0$: M342 is uniformly distributed across positions, so
$P(\text{terminal}) = 1/\bar{L}$ where $\bar{L}$ is the mean
sequence length.
$H_1$: M342 is preferentially terminal.

\begin{table}[htbp]
\centering
\caption{Analysis 1 --- Suffix Theorem ($N = 2{,}742$)}
\begin{tabular}{lr}
\toprule
Metric & Value \\
\midrule
Total M342 occurrences          & 1,190 \\
Terminal occurrences            & 809 \quad (68.0\%) \\
Initial occurrences             & 1 \quad ($<$0.1\%) \\
Mean sequence length $\bar{L}$  & 4.13 \\
Null probability $1/\bar{L}$    & 0.2421 \\
Binomial $p$-value (one-tailed) & $\mathbf{1.70 \times 10^{-222}}$ \\
\midrule
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\paragraph{Note on rate.}
The terminal rate of 68.0\% (vs.\ 100\% in the 41-seal pilot corpus)
reflects the broader corpus including tablet and pottery inscriptions
where M342 sometimes appears in medial position (31.9\% of occurrences,
$n = 381$).
To test whether the medial occurrences are explained by multi-line
artifact encoding, we decompose by artifact type using the
\texttt{inscobj} field from \texttt{indusarrays}
(Table~\ref{tab:m342medial}).

\begin{table}[htbp]
\centering
\caption{M342 positional distribution by artifact type ($N_{\text{M342}} = 1{,}190$)}
\label{tab:m342medial}
\begin{tabular}{lrrrrrr}
\toprule
Artifact Type & Total & Terminal & Term\% & Medial & Med\% & Initial \\
\midrule
Seals              & 720 & 548 & 76.1\% & 172 & 23.9\% & 0 \\
Sealings           & 223 & 129 & 57.8\% &  94 & 42.2\% & 0 \\
Tablets/Implements & 221 & 111 & 50.2\% & 110 & 49.8\% & 0 \\
Pottery/Graffiti   &  24 &  19 & 79.2\% &   5 & 20.8\% & 0 \\
Other              &   2 &   2 &100.0\% &   0 &  0.0\% & 0 \\
\midrule
Total              &1,190 & 809 & 68.0\% & 381 & 32.0\% & 0 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The multi-line hypothesis holds strongly for tablets and implements
(49.8\% medial), where multi-face encoding is expected.
However, seals---nominally single-face objects---still show 23.9\%
(172 occurrences) medial M342.
Many seals in the Mahadevan concordance carry text on multiple faces
(obverse, reverse, boss); M342 may close the obverse line while
inscription continues on the boss.
We cannot verify this from sequence data alone without per-face artifact
records.
We therefore acknowledge that M342 \emph{occasionally functions in a
medial slot} and the medial-terminal distinction for M342 requires
physical artifact-level verification.
The terminal bias remains highly significant across all artifact types
($p = 1.70 \times 10^{-222}$) and M342 never appears in initial
position ($n = 1$ out of 1,190 tokens; $<$0.1\%).
The binomial $p$-value at full scale is astronomically more significant
($10^{-222}$ vs.\ $10^{-21}$).

\paragraph{Independent validation.}
ICIT \citep{Fuls2010} labels Wells~520 ($=$ M342) as TMK (Terminal
Marker), assigned from frequency statistics without reference to any
phonetic hypothesis.

\section{Analysis 2 --- Positional Entropy}
\label{sec:analysis2}
% ============================================================

\paragraph{Hypothesis.}
A grammatically constrained script shows lower Shannon entropy at
terminal positions than at initial positions.

\paragraph{Test.}
Compute Shannon entropy $H$ at each sign slot across all 2,742
inscriptions. Compare $H_0$ (initial slot) vs.\ $H_1$ (second slot).

\begin{table}[htbp]
\centering
\caption{Analysis 2 --- Positional Entropy ($N = 2{,}742$)}
\begin{tabular}{lr}
\toprule
Metric & Value \\
\midrule
Entropy at position 0 (initial)    & 6.579 bits \\
Entropy at position 1              & 6.021 bits \\
Entropy drop (position 0 $\to$ 1)  & \textbf{0.558 bits} \\
Threshold for confirmation         & $\geq$0.3 bits \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The 0.558-bit drop means position~0 carries approximately 1.5$\times$
more variability than position~1.
Entropy generally decreases across positions 0--7
(6.579, 6.021, 6.124, 5.985, 5.708, 5.502, 5.181, 5.073 bits),
with local variance at position~2 ($6.124 > 6.021$),
consistent with an open initial vocabulary converging toward a
constrained terminal grammar.
\emph{This proof was pending at pilot scale ($N = 41$); it is now
confirmed at $N = 2{,}742$.}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.82\linewidth]{fig1_entropy.pdf}
\caption{Positional entropy profile across sign slots 0--7
($N = 2{,}742$ inscriptions). The 0.558-bit drop from position~0
to position~1 is the mathematical signature of an open initial
vocabulary constrained by a terminal grammar. Entropy declines
monotonically from position~2 onward.}

\section{Analysis 3 --- Co-occurrence Cluster}
\label{sec:analysis3}
% ============================================================

\paragraph{Hypothesis.}
Grammatically related signs co-occur more often than their individual
frequencies predict.

\paragraph{Test.}
Chi-square test on the three highest-frequency signs that interact with
the terminal marker: M267, M99, M342.
Expected co-occurrence computed under independence:
$E = N \times P(\text{M267}) \times P(\text{M99}) \times P(\text{M342})$.

\begin{table}[htbp]
\centering
\caption{Analysis 3 --- Co-occurrence Cluster ($N = 2{,}742$)}
\begin{tabular}{lr}
\toprule
Metric & Value \\
\midrule
Cluster signs       & M267, M99, M342 \\
Observed co-occurrence & 142 inscriptions \\
Expected under independence & 25.8 \\
Observed / Expected  & $5.5\times$ \\
$\chi^2$             & \textbf{523.3} \\
$p$-value            & $\mathbf{\approx 0}$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\emph{This proof was pending at pilot scale; it is now confirmed.}
The cluster M267--M99--M342 appears in 142 inscriptions spanning
all five major sites, confirming the pattern is not site-specific.

\section{Analysis 4 --- Agglutination Ratio}
\label{sec:analysis4}
% ============================================================

\paragraph{Hypothesis.}
If M342 is a grammatical suffix, specific signs (roots or stems) should
attach to it at rates far exceeding statistical independence.

\paragraph{Test.}
For all signs $a$ that precede M342, compute the observed/expected ratio:
\[
    r(a) = \frac{\text{count}(a \to \text{M342})}
                {P(a) \cdot P(\text{M342}) \cdot N_{\text{bigrams}}}
\]

\begin{table}[htbp]
\centering
\caption{Analysis 4 --- Agglutination Ratio ($N = 2{,}742$)}
\begin{tabular}{lr}
\toprule
Metric & Value \\
\midrule
Top predecessor sign                  & M48 \\
Observed M48$\to$M342 bigrams         & 105 \\
Expected under independence           & 11.7 \\
Agglutination ratio                   & $\mathbf{8.97\times}$ \\
Min.\ frequency threshold             & 3 occurrences \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\paragraph{Directional note.}
Because direction was not available in the corpus export and RTL is
assumed, sign M48 is recorded as appearing \emph{before} M342 in
the stored array, which corresponds to the sign physically to the
\emph{right} of M342 on the seal face.
The existence of preferential attachment ($8.97\times$) is robust
regardless of direction; the functional label ``preceding'' reflects
array order, not necessarily phonological precedence.

\section{Analysis 6 --- Cross-Site Markov Consistency}
\label{sec:crosssite}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Design}

If the script is a \emph{local} symbol system (guild marks, clan
heraldry, administrative tokens), each site would develop independent
statistical regularities.
If it is a \emph{standardized} writing system, inscriptions from
geographically distant sites should follow the same transition grammar.

We test this by training a bigram Markov matrix on Mohenjo-daro
inscriptions ($n = 1{,}347$) and scoring all other sites using
per-bigram log-likelihood against that matrix.
Bigram matrices capture first-order dependencies only; they serve
as a conservative baseline---the strong cross-site $Z = 50.12$
indicates that even first-order transition regularities are highly
consistent across sites, a property not observed in non-linguistic
symbol systems.
Scores are compared against a
random-shuffle baseline.

\paragraph{Baseline.}
For 1,000 trials, the signs within each Harappa inscription are randomly
permuted (preserving the sign inventory but destroying order).
Each permuted corpus is scored against the Mohenjo-daro matrix.
The baseline distribution gives the log-likelihood expected under no
grammatical relationship.

\subsection{Geographic Gradient}

Every site scores substantially better than the random baseline.
The fit generally degrades with geographic distance from
Mohenjo-daro (Table~\ref{tab:crosssite}): Harappa ($+6.19$),
Lothal ($+6.01$) and Chanhudaro ($+5.29$)---both falling within a
tight band ($\approx +5.3$--$6.0$) suggesting broad regional
attenuation rather than strict linear decay---then Kalibangan ($+4.05$),
West Asian trade outposts ($+1.08$).
The aggregated ``Other Sites'' category ($+5.34$) scores above
Kalibangan, breaking strict monotonic decay; this category likely
represents a mix of intermediate-distance locales rather than a
coherent geographic unit.

This spatial decay pattern is inconsistent with random marking or
local guild symbology, which would produce site-independent scores.
It is consistent with a standardized institutional writing system
originating at or near Mohenjo-daro and propagating along trade
networks, with grammar regularity attenuating at the periphery.

West Asian finds comprise only 13 inscriptions.
Small samples yield noisier log-likelihood estimates; the lower score
there may partially reflect sampling variance rather than geographic

However, a score of $+1.08$ above random (vs.\ $+6.19$ for Harappa)
confirms that the Mohenjo-daro grammar is present even at trade
outposts 1,500~km distant.
This caveat should be noted in any peer-review submission.

\section{Analysis 7 --- Direction Grammar Test}
\label{sec:direction}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Motivation}

The v1.5 paper carried an acknowledged limitation: direction data was
absent from the corpus export, so all inscriptions were assumed RTL.
This section resolves that limitation using direction codes scraped
from the \texttt{indusarrays} Firestore collection of
\texttt{indusscript.in} (3,916 records covering textnums 1001--9905).

\subsection{Direction Assignment}

Each textnum is assigned a single direction via priority rule:
sideline~0 (only side of artifact) wins; else sideline~1 (first
side); else majority vote across sides.
Direction codes follow Mahadevan/ICIT conventions: 1 = RTL, 2 = LTR,
3 = single-sign line, 4 = symmetrical, 5 = doubtful, 9 = no text.

\begin{tabular}{lrr}

Direction & Inscriptions & \% \\
\midrule

Other/unknown & 207 &  7.5\% \\
\midrule
Total & 2,742 & 100\% \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\subsection{Test Design}

We train the bigram transition matrix on all 2,360 RTL inscriptions
(378 unique signs, 2,443 significant bigrams) and score the 175 LTR
inscriptions against it.
Add-epsilon smoothing ($\varepsilon = 10^{-6}$) handles unseen bigrams.
We compare three perplexity values: RTL in-sample (self-score),
LTR against RTL matrix, and a random baseline
(1,000 permutations of LTR sign sequences scored against RTL matrix).

\subsection{Interpretation}

Two findings stand in tension and both are true:

\paragraph{Finding A --- Grammar is NOT direction-invariant.}
LTR perplexity (97.68) is $13.3\times$ higher than RTL in-sample
(7.34).
Exact bigram transition probabilities degrade when inscription
direction flips.
LTR inscriptions follow a distinct word order from RTL.

\paragraph{Finding B --- Statistical dependencies survive the flip.}
LTR perplexity (97.68) is 7,887$\times$ lower than random permutations
(770,403), significant at $p = 1.03 \times 10^{-54}$, Cohen's
$d = 1.634$.
The ``glue'' between signs --- hub-sign co-occurrences, bigram
affinities --- is so strong that even reversed-direction sequences
score far above noise under an RTL-trained matrix.

\paragraph{Consequence for earlier proofs.}
The RTL assumption in Proofs 1--6 is now confirmed for 86.1\% of
inscriptions.
The 175 LTR inscriptions (6.4\%) introduce a modest noise signal in
bigram statistics, but because LTR perplexity is 7,887$\times$ above
random under RTL grammar, the directional noise is bounded: it cannot
artificially create the $Z = 50.12$ cross-site consistency or the
$\chi^2 = 523$ co-occurrence cluster.
The RTL assumption limitation in prior versions of this paper is
\textbf{resolved}.

\section{Punctuation Null Test}
\label{sec:punct}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Threat Model}

\citet{Farmer2004} and \citet{Sproat2014} argue that a terminal sign
with high terminal rate is consistent with a scribal period mark.
A period mark also produces 100\% terminal rate by definition.

\subsection{Counter-Test}

Punctuation attaches uniformly to all preceding signs.
A grammatical suffix is \emph{selective}: elevated bigram frequency
with specific roots, near-chance with others.
We measure selectivity via the Coefficient of Variation (CV) of
bigram elevations across all qualifying preceding signs:
\[
\mathrm{CV} = \frac{\sigma_{\mathrm{elevation}}}{\mu_{\mathrm{elevation}}}
\]
A CV well above the range typical of uniform delimiters (empirically
$<0.3$ in analogous symbol systems) indicates selective attachment
inconsistent with punctuation behavior; a high CV ($>0.7$) is the
signature of a grammatical suffix that bonds preferentially with
specific roots.

Additionally, removing a suffix releases the terminal slot, increasing
terminal entropy.
Removing punctuation does not.

\subsection{Results}

\begin{table}[htbp]
\centering
\caption{Punctuation Null Test results ($N = 2{,}742$)}
\begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{lrX}
\toprule
Metric & Value & Interpretation \\
\midrule
Signs evaluated ($\geq$3 occ.)  & 70   & --- \\
Mean bigram elevation            & $3.84\times$ & --- \\
Std.\ dev.\ elevation            & $2.92\times$ & --- \\
Selectivity Index (CV)           & \textbf{0.761} & High $\Rightarrow$ suffix-like \\
Terminal entropy \emph{with} M342    & 4.646 bits & --- \\
Terminal entropy \emph{without} M342 & 5.350 bits & --- \\
Entropy delta on removal         & $\mathbf{+0.703}$ \textbf{bits} & Suffix behaviour \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabularx}
\end{table}

A scribal period produces entropy delta $\approx 0$ on removal.
M342 produces $+0.703$ bits on the full 2,742-inscription corpus,
meaning it actively suppresses competing signs from the terminal
slot---the mathematical signature of a grammatical morpheme.
Note: the delta is smaller than the 41-seal pilot ($+1.77$ bits)
because the full corpus includes tablet and pottery inscriptions where
the terminal slot is more varied; the direction of the effect is
unchanged and the CV of 0.761 remains well above the range
consistent with uniform delimiter behavior.

\section{Motif--Sign Correlation}
\label{sec:motif}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Method}

Motif data (field symbol code \texttt{fs80} from the \texttt{indusarrays}
Firestore collection) was joined to 2,742 corpus inscriptions via
textnum.
For each top-PMI sign pair, we compute the motif distribution among
inscriptions containing both signs and compare it to the corpus-wide
background distribution using a chi-square test.

\subsection{Primary Finding: M172--M304 Kalibangan Lock}

The highest-PMI pair in our network is M172--M304 (PMI\,=\,7.94,
$n_{\text{co-occ}} = 7$).
All 7 co-occurrences appear on UNICORN seals (fs80 = 13);
all 7 occur at Kalibangan.
Every inscription in this set carries the identical formula:

\[
[\text{M1}, \text{M1}, \text{M121}, \text{M121}, \text{M99},
 \text{M172}, \text{M304}, \text{M307}]
\]

This is a \emph{frozen formula}: zero sequence variation across 7
physically distinct artifacts from one site.
Background UNICORN rate: 34.6\%.
Observed rate for M172--M304: 100\%.
Enrichment: $2.9\times$ over base.

Frozen formulae with PMI $>7$ indicate institutional standardization
rather than phonetic or semantic variation.
This pattern is consistent with an institutionally fixed formula
specific to Kalibangan UNICORN seals; whether it encodes an
administrative, cultic, or ownership function cannot be determined
from structural data.
No phonetic inference is made.

\section{Archaeological Correlation: Keezhadi}
\label{sec:keezhadi}
% ============================================================

\citet{Rajan2025}: \textit{Indus Signs and Graffiti Marks of Tamil Nadu:
A Morphological Study}. Dept.\ of Archaeology, Govt.\ of Tamil Nadu.
Publication No.\ 357. ISBN 978-81-977842-5-5.

The survey documents 14,165 graffiti-bearing sherds from 140 Tamil Nadu
sites; 60\% of IVC base signs have exact parallels; 90\%+ of all graffiti
marks have IVC parallels.
Both anchor signs (jar M342, fish M149) are physically present at
Keeladi (2,132 sherds each).

\paragraph{Independent shape convergence: M304 and M328.}
An independent convergence is noted with \citet{Rajan2025}, who
mapped Tamil Nadu Iron Age graffiti signs to Mahadevan numbers.
The signs our algorithms isolated as structurally distinctive---M328
(90.1\% Harappa-concentrated, 85.9\% terminal) and M304 (co-occurs
with M172 in an identical formula on all 7 attested Kalibangan
UNICORN seals, PMI\,=\,7.94)---appear as exact shape-matches in
Tamil Nadu graffiti (Rajan sign numbers 32.0 and 31.0 respectively).
That signs computationally isolated as structurally distinctive are
also visually identifiable in an independent dataset is consistent
with those signs having stable, non-arbitrary graphic forms.
This does not validate the Markov or PMI analyses; it confirms only
the visual distinctiveness of the targeted sign shapes.
No phonetic inference and no claim of morphological or functional
continuity follows from this convergence.

\paragraph{Temporal gap.}
IVC ended $\sim$1900 \BCE; Keeladi dates to $\sim$600 \BCE: a gap
of $\sim$1,300 years.
Morphological overlap does not establish direct descent.
The Megalithic Black-and-Red Ware pottery tradition shows graffiti
marks at sites across this period \citep[][pp.\ 13--14]{Rajan2025};
whether this represents continuous transmission or independent
recurrence of similar forms cannot be determined without
sequence-level comparative analysis.
Whether sign \emph{transition probabilities} are preserved across the
gap cannot be tested computationally until dated sequence data from
stratified Megalithic contexts are available in machine-readable form.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ============================================================

We have presented nine statistically independent structural tests on
2,742 inscriptions from the Indus Valley Civilisation.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{M342 is positionally constrained as a terminal morpheme}
          ($p = 1.70 \times 10^{-222}$), independently confirmed by ICIT's
          TMK label.
    \item \textbf{Positional entropy drops 0.558 bits} from initial to
          second slot, confirming an open initial vocabulary with
          terminal grammatical constraint.
    \item \textbf{Signs M267, M99, M342 cluster at $5.5\times$ above
          chance} ($\chi^2 = 523$, $p \approx 0$), ruling out random
          co-occurrence.
    \item \textbf{Sign M48 attaches to M342 at $8.97\times$ above
          independence}, consistent with agglutinative suffix attachment.
    \item \textbf{398 signs partition into three functional classes}
          (terminal-biased, initial-biased, medial-locked) with no
          cross-boundary signs above noise, consistent with morphological
          role differentiation.
    \item \textbf{The Mohenjo-daro grammar generalises to all sites}
          ($Z = 50.12$, $p = 0$), with fit generally decaying
          with geographic distance---a pattern consistent with a
          standardized institutional writing system, not local iconography.
    \item \textbf{The RTL assumption is resolved}: direction data
          from \texttt{indusarrays} confirms 86.1\% RTL. LTR
          inscriptions score 7,887$\times$ below random under RTL
          grammar ($p = 1.03 \times 10^{-54}$), bounding directional
          noise in Proofs 1--6.
    \item \textbf{Sign M113 is $37.9\times$ positionally enriched on
          elephant-motif seals} and occupies the MEDIAL slot in a
          recurrent formula across Harappa and Lothal, structurally
          distinguishing it from terminal and initial signs; no
          semantic category is assigned.
          Sign pair M172--M304 appears in an identical eight-sign
          sequence on all 7 attested Kalibangan UNICORN seals
          (PMI\,=\,7.94, zero sequence variation; $n = 7$,
          single site), consistent with a high-constraint
          institutional formula; no semantic or titular content
          is inferred.
    \item \textbf{154 structural substitution synonyms} are recovered.
          M328 (90.1\% Harappa-concentrated), M93 (100\%
          Chanhudaro-concentrated), and M172 (87.5\%
          Kalibangan-concentrated) are terminal-biased signs with
          strong single-site concentration.
          M328, M12, and M343 are confirmed positional equivalents
          (cross-site slot substitutions in identical formulae).
          Signs M304 and M328 have exact shape-matches in Tamil Nadu
          Iron Age graffiti \citep{Rajan2025}, confirming the visual
          distinctiveness of these computationally isolated sign forms;
          no morphological or functional continuity is implied.
\end{enumerate}

The hypothesis that the Indus Valley Script encodes random marks,
administrative tokens, or site-specific guild symbology is statistically
untenable given these results.
The structural properties observed---positional constraint, selective
suffix attachment, medial-positional bias, and cross-site grammatical
standardisation---are consistent with a language encoding system and
inconsistent with random or purely iconic marking; however,
distinguishing a structured proto-writing system from a full language
encoding system requires phonetic evidence beyond the scope of this
study.
